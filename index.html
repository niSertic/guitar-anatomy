How an Electric Guitar Works

Strings, magnetism, and electronics — turning mechanical vibration into sound.

Magnetized Strings

Each pickup is a coil of fine wire wound thousands of times around permanent magnet pole pieces. The magnets impose a fixed magnetic field on the steel strings above them, effectively making a short length of each string part of the magnetic circuit. At rest, the field through the coil is steady and no signal is produced.

Induction: Vibration Becomes Voltage

When a plucked string vibrates, it moves the magnetized region through the coil's magnetic field. By Faraday's law of induction, a changing magnetic flux through a coil induces a voltage across it. The string's motion generates a small alternating current that mirrors the vibration — its frequency sets the pitch, and the complex waveform of fundamental plus harmonics carries the timbre that makes the instrument recognizable.

Shaping the Signal

This raw signal is weak and high-impedance. Before it leaves the guitar, the pickup selector chooses which coil or combination feeds the circuit, the volume potentiometer sets its level, and the tone control bleeds high frequencies to ground through a capacitor. The result is a shaped audio signal of only a few hundred millivolts — far too quiet to drive a speaker on its own.

Amplification and Sound

The instrument cable carries the signal to an amplifier, which boosts its voltage and current enough to move a loudspeaker cone. The cone displaces air in step with the original waveform, recreating the string's vibration as pressure waves — the sound you hear. Every stage from string to speaker preserves the same waveform; the amplifier simply makes it powerful enough to be heard.
